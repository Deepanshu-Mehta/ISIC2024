\begin{abstract}
We present a three-phase machine learning pipeline for binary
classification of malignant versus benign skin lesions using the
ISIC~2024 SLICE-3D dataset~\cite{isic2024}.
The dataset contains 401,059 lesion records from 1,042 patients with
393 malignant cases (0.098\%), posing extreme class imbalance and
patient-group leakage challenges.
\textbf{Phase~1} (tabular) combines clinically motivated ABCDE feature
engineering, patient-wise ugly duckling z-scores, and a GBDT ensemble
(LightGBM, XGBoost, CatBoost) with isotonic calibration, achieving
pAUC~=~0.1672.
\textbf{Phase~2} (image) fine-tunes four pretrained vision backbones
(EfficientNetV2-S, EVA02-Small, ConvNeXtV2-B, SwinV2-B) on 401K lesion
images using focal loss, weighted sampling, and test-time augmentation
(TTA) with D4 symmetry transforms; the best individual image model
(SwinV2-B) achieves pAUC~=~0.1549.
\textbf{Phase~3} (stacking) combines all five out-of-fold (OOF)
prediction sources through rank transformation and a logistic regression
meta-learner, achieving a final \textbf{pAUC~=~0.1745} ---
a 5.6\% improvement over the best individual model.
All phases share identical cross-validation splits
(\texttt{StratifiedGroupKFold}, $k{=}5$, grouped by patient) to ensure
leak-free OOF alignment for stacking.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Melanoma is responsible for the vast majority of skin cancer deaths
despite comprising only $\sim$1\% of diagnoses~\cite{siegel2024cancer}.
Five-year survival drops from 98\% (localised) to 32\% (stage~IV),
making early detection critical.
The ISIC~2024 SLICE-3D challenge~\cite{isic2024} provides both
large-scale structured lesion measurements extracted from Canfield's
Vectra Total Body Photography~(TBP) system and dermoscopic images,
enabling multi-modal approaches.

This paper describes a three-phase pipeline:
(i)~tabular GBDT ensemble on structured metadata,
(ii)~fine-tuned pretrained vision backbones on lesion images, and
(iii)~a stacking meta-learner that fuses predictions from both modalities.
The primary metric is partial AUC above 80\% TPR (pAUC, range $[0, 0.2]$).

The \texttt{tbp\_lv\_*} column family is not arbitrary metadata:
it numerically encodes the canonical ABCDE clinical criteria~\cite{friedman1985}
as machine-extracted measurements (Table~\ref{tab:abcde}), making
the metadata directly actionable for melanoma screening.

\paragraph*{Contributions}
\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep, leftmargin=*]
  \item A rigorous leakage audit removing columns that inflate training
        pAUC to 0.197 yet fail at inference.
  \item A staged feature pipeline
        (55~$\to$~45~$\to$~58~$\to$~271~$\to$~88 columns) with ABCDE
        hand-crafted features and ugly duckling patient-relative z-scores.
  \item Diagnosis and fix of LightGBM's count-based
        \texttt{min\_child\_samples} under-performing with
        \texttt{scale\_pos\_weight}, raising pAUC from 0.1217 to 0.1400.
  \item A multi-architecture image model sweep across four backbone
        families (CNN, ViT, ConvNet, hybrid) with focal loss and D4 TTA.
  \item A stacking meta-learner achieving pAUC~=~0.1745 by fusing
        tabular and image signals through rank-transformed features.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{ISIC Challenges}
The ISIC has hosted annual dermoscopy classification challenges since
2016~\cite{codella2018}. While earlier editions focused on CNNs for
image classification, the 2024 edition introduced TBP metadata,
enabling tabular ML approaches. Top solutions combined image and
tabular features through stacking or late fusion.

\paragraph{GBDTs for Tabular Data}
GBDTs remain the dominant paradigm for structured data~\cite{grinsztajn2022},
consistently outperforming deep learning on medium-sized tabular
benchmarks. LightGBM~\cite{ke2017lightgbm},
XGBoost~\cite{chen2016xgboost}, and CatBoost~\cite{prokhorenkova2018catboost}
offer complementary inductive biases (leaf-wise vs.~level-wise vs.~ordered
boosting).

\paragraph{Transfer Learning for Medical Imaging}
Fine-tuning ImageNet-pretrained backbones is the dominant paradigm for
medical image classification~\cite{russakovsky2015imagenet}.
EfficientNetV2~\cite{tan2021efficientnetv2} introduced progressive
training and fused-MBConv blocks for efficiency.
Vision Transformers (ViT)~\cite{dosovitskiy2021vit} and their variants
--- EVA02~\cite{fang2023eva02} with masked image modelling pretraining,
SwinV2~\cite{liu2022swinv2} with shifted window attention, and
ConvNeXtV2~\cite{woo2023convnextv2} modernising pure convolutions ---
have achieved state-of-the-art results across vision benchmarks.
For skin lesion classification specifically, ensemble diversity across
architecture families (CNN, ViT, hybrid) is more valuable than
single-model accuracy~\cite{cassidy2022analysis}.

\paragraph{Focal Loss}
Lin et al.~\cite{lin2017focal} proposed focal loss to down-weight
easy negatives in object detection.
For binary classification with extreme imbalance, focal loss modulates
the standard cross-entropy:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{focal}} = -\alpha_t (1 - p_t)^\gamma \log(p_t)
  \label{eq:focal}
\end{equation}
where $\gamma$ controls focus on hard examples and $\alpha_t$ balances
classes. We use $\gamma{=}2$, $\alpha{=}0.25$.

\paragraph{Probability Calibration}
Modern tree ensembles often produce poorly calibrated
probabilities~\cite{niculescu2005}. Isotonic
regression~\cite{zadrozny2002} is non-parametric and monotone;
Platt scaling~\cite{platt1999} applies a sigmoid fit;
temperature scaling~\cite{guo2017} divides logits by a learned
scalar~$T$.

\paragraph{Stacking and Model Fusion}
Stacking~\cite{wolpert1992stacked} trains a meta-learner on
out-of-fold predictions from base models. Rank-averaging converts
heterogeneous probability scales to a common percentile space,
preserving rank-based metrics like AUC~\cite{caruana2004ensemble}.
For extremely low positive counts, simple linear meta-learners
outperform tree-based stackers due to lower overfitting risk.

\subsection{Competition Metric: Partial AUC}

The official scoring metric is the partial area under the ROC curve
above a minimum TPR threshold $\tau = 0.80$:
\begin{equation}
  \pauc = \int_{\tau}^{1}
  \frac{d\tpr}{d\fpr}\,d\tpr, \qquad \pauc \in [0,\,1-\tau]
  \label{eq:pauc}
\end{equation}
The Kaggle implementation uses label-flipping and prediction negation:
\begin{equation}
  \pauc(y, \hat{p}) = \text{AUC}\!\left(
    1-y,\;{-\hat{p}},\;\text{max\_fpr} = 1-\tau
  \right)
  \label{eq:pauc_flip}
\end{equation}
This differs from \texttt{sklearn}'s \texttt{roc\_auc\_score(max\_fpr=0.2)},
which applies the McClish normalisation~\cite{mcclish1989} and yields
systematically different values.

\subsection{Cross-Validation Strategy}

All three phases use identical \texttt{StratifiedGroupKFold}~($k{=}5$,
\texttt{seed=42})~\cite{sklearn} with groups~=~\texttt{patient\_id}.
No patient appears in both training and validation within a fold,
preventing group leakage from the $\approx$385 lesions per patient.
This shared CV strategy ensures that OOF predictions from all phases
are aligned by \texttt{isic\_id} for leak-free stacking in Phase~3.

\subsection{Leakage Audit}

Table~\ref{tab:leakage} lists columns removed before modelling.
The subtlest case is \texttt{has\_lesion\_id}: all 393 malignant
training rows have a non-null \texttt{lesion\_id} (biopsied
lesions), giving pAUC $=$ 0.197 in training.
The test set contains no \texttt{lesion\_id} column, however, so
the model would output near-zero probability for every test row ---
a classic leakage trap.

\subsection{Preprocessing (55 $\to$ 45 Columns)}

The preprocessor: (i)~removes 12 columns (Table~\ref{tab:leakage}),
(ii)~label-encodes five categoricals
(\texttt{sex}, \texttt{anatom\_site\_general}, \texttt{attribution},
\texttt{tbp\_tile\_type}, \texttt{tbp\_lv\_location\_simple}),
(iii)~median-imputes \texttt{age\_approx} (1.7\% missing) and adds an
\texttt{age\_approx\_missing} indicator,
and (iv)~fills remaining NaN with~0.

\subsection{ABCDE Feature Engineering (45 $\to$ 58 Columns)}

Thirteen domain-motivated features are added. Selected equations:

\textit{Colour~(C) --- CIELAB distance and chroma:}
\begin{align}
  \Delta E &= \sqrt{(\Delta A)^2 + (\Delta B)^2 + (\Delta L)^2}
  \label{eq:deltae}\\
  \text{chroma} &= \sqrt{A_{\text{lesion}}^2 + B_{\text{lesion}}^2}
  \label{eq:chroma}
\end{align}

\textit{Asymmetry / Border~(A,~B) --- shape descriptors:}
\begin{align}
  \text{circularity} &= \frac{4\pi\cdot\text{area}}
    {\text{perimeter}^2 + \varepsilon}
  \label{eq:circ}\\
  \text{border\_complexity} &= \frac{\text{perimeter}}
    {\sqrt{\text{area}} + \varepsilon}
  \label{eq:border}
\end{align}
where $\varepsilon = 10^{-8}$ guards against zero denominators.

\textit{Interaction features:}
\[
  \text{age\_x\_area} = \text{age} \times \text{areaMM}^2,
  \quad
  \text{ecc} \times \Delta E
\]

\subsection{Ugly Duckling Features (58 $\to$ 271 Columns)}

The \emph{ugly duckling sign}~\cite{grob1998ugly} states that a mole
markedly different from a patient's other lesions warrants biopsy.
For each feature $f$ and grouping $G$:
\begin{equation}
  z_{G}(f) = \frac{f - \mu_{G}(f)}{\sigma_{G}(f) + \varepsilon},
  \qquad
  r_{G}(f) = \text{ECDF}_{G}(f)
  \label{eq:ud}
\end{equation}
Singleton groups set $z_G = 0$ and $r_G = 0.5$ (centred, neutral).
Three grouping levels from the 2nd-place competition solution:
\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lp{3.5cm}}
  \toprule
  \textbf{Group} & \textbf{Clinical question} \\
  \midrule
  \texttt{[patient\_id]} & Weird vs.\ all patient moles? \\
  \texttt{[patient\_id, site]} & Weird for this body region? \\
  \texttt{[patient\_id, location]} & Weird for this TBP location? \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
Three aggregates are also computed: $\text{ud\_outlier\_count} =
\sum_f\mathbf{1}[|z_\text{pat}(f)|>2]$, mean absolute z-score,
and a single-lesion-patient flag.

\subsection{Feature Selection (271 $\to$ 88 Columns)}

Three sequential filters:
(1)~exclusion list (\texttt{patient\_id}, \texttt{target}, \texttt{has\_lesion\_id}),
(2)~quasi-constant filter (single value $> 99.5\%$ of rows), and
(3)~correlation filter ($|\rho| > 0.90$; threshold validated at
0.85/0.90/0.95 in EDA notebook, with 0.90 selected).

\section{Phase~1: Tabular Models}

\subsection{Seed Averaging}

Each GBDT is trained with seeds $\{42, 123, 456, 789, 2024\}$ and
predictions are averaged per fold, reducing variance from stochastic
gradient descent paths.

\subsection{LightGBM~\cite{ke2017lightgbm}}

Key hyperparameters: \texttt{num\_leaves=63}, \texttt{max\_depth=6},
\texttt{learning\_rate=0.05}, up to 2000 trees with 100-round early
stopping, $L_1 = L_2 = 0.1$, \texttt{scale\_pos\_weight=100}.

\paragraph{Critical imbalance fix}
\texttt{min\_child\_samples} is a \emph{count-based} constraint that
ignores \texttt{scale\_pos\_weight}: with $\approx$314 positives per
fold and \texttt{min\_child\_samples=80}, LightGBM can form at most
$\lfloor 314/80 \rfloor = 3$ pure positive leaves.
XGBoost's \texttt{min\_child\_weight} is \emph{hessian-sum based}:
under logistic loss with \texttt{scale\_pos\_weight=100}, a single
positive sample contributes $\approx 25$ to the hessian sum, easily
satisfying \texttt{min\_child\_weight=10}.

The fix sets:
\begin{equation}
  \texttt{min\_sum\_hessian\_in\_leaf} = 10.0
  \label{eq:mchw}
\end{equation}
This raised pAUC from 0.1217 to \textbf{0.1400} and halved the
standard deviation (0.0132 $\to$ 0.0052).

\subsection{XGBoost~\cite{chen2016xgboost}}

\texttt{max\_depth=6}, \texttt{min\_child\_weight=10},
\texttt{subsample=colsample\_bytree=0.8}, $\alpha=\lambda=0.1$,
\texttt{scale\_pos\_weight=100}.
The \texttt{max\_delta\_step=1} parameter clips leaf weight updates to
$[-1, +1]$, preventing divergence under extreme imbalance.

\subsection{CatBoost~\cite{prokhorenkova2018catboost}}

\texttt{depth=6}, \texttt{learning\_rate=0.05}, \texttt{l2\_leaf\_reg=3.0},
up to 2000 iterations with early stopping.
Class imbalance is handled via \texttt{auto\_class\_weights=SqrtBalanced}
($\sqrt{N_\text{neg}/N_\text{pos}}$ weighting).

\subsection{SVM Baseline (Course Requirement)~\cite{cortes1995svm}}

An SVM with RBF kernel satisfies the course requirement for a classical
probabilistic model.
Pipeline: \texttt{StandardScaler} $\to$ \texttt{SVC(probability=True,
class\_weight=balanced)}, where \texttt{probability=True} enables Platt
scaling~\cite{platt1999}.
Training uses a 20K stratified subsample per fold ($\mathcal{O}(n^3)$
prohibitive at full scale).
The SVM is excluded from the final ensemble due to high variance
(pAUC std $= 0.0245$).

\subsection{Rank-Averaging Ensemble}

Rank-averaging is robust to probability scale differences across models:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{r}_m = \frac{\text{rank}(\hat{p}_m)}{N+1},
  \qquad
  \hat{p}_{\text{ens}} = \frac{1}{3}\sum_{m=1}^{3}\hat{r}_m
  \label{eq:rankavg}
\end{equation}
where the sum is over LightGBM, XGBoost, and CatBoost (SVM excluded).

\subsection{Isotonic Calibration~\cite{niculescu2005}}

Isotonic regression is fitted on the full set of OOF ensemble
predictions.
As a monotone transformation, calibration preserves score rankings and
therefore cannot decrease pAUC.

\section{Phase~2: Image Deep Learning}
\label{sec:phase2}

Phase~2 fine-tunes pretrained vision backbones on 401K lesion images
to capture visual patterns (texture, border irregularity, colour
heterogeneity) not represented in the tabular metadata.

\subsection{Architecture}

We use the \texttt{timm} library~\cite{rw2019timm} to load
ImageNet-pretrained backbones. The classification head replaces the
original with: \texttt{Dropout}(0.3) $\to$ \texttt{Linear}($d$, 1),
where $d$ is the backbone's feature dimension. The sigmoid output
produces a malignancy probability.

Table~\ref{tab:backbones} lists the four backbones selected to
maximise architectural diversity:

\begin{table}[!t]
  \centering
  \caption{Phase~2 Backbone Architectures.}
  \label{tab:backbones}
  \renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.1}
  \begin{tabular}{llcc}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Backbone} & \textbf{Family} & \textbf{Params} & \textbf{Input} \\
    \midrule
    EfficientNetV2-S & CNN (MBConv) & 20M & $224^2$ \\
    EVA02-Small      & ViT (MIM pretrain) & 22M & $336^2$ \\
    ConvNeXtV2-B     & Pure ConvNet & 88M & $224^2$ \\
    SwinV2-B         & Shifted-window ViT & 87M & $256^2$ \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

\paragraph{Architecture selection rationale}
The four backbones span three distinct inductive bias families:
(1)~\textbf{EfficientNetV2-S}~\cite{tan2021efficientnetv2} uses
fused-MBConv blocks with strong local spatial priors;
(2)~\textbf{EVA02-Small}~\cite{fang2023eva02} is a plain ViT pretrained
with masked image modelling (MIM), providing global self-attention
from the first layer;
(3)~\textbf{ConvNeXtV2-B}~\cite{woo2023convnextv2} modernises pure
convolutions with FCMAE pretraining and Global Response Normalisation;
(4)~\textbf{SwinV2-B}~\cite{liu2022swinv2} uses shifted-window attention
with log-spaced continuous position bias, combining local and global
reasoning hierarchically.
This diversity ensures the image models make \emph{different errors},
which is critical for stacking to improve over any individual
model~\cite{caruana2004ensemble}.

\subsection{Training Configuration}

\paragraph{Loss function}
Focal loss (Eq.~\ref{eq:focal}) with $\gamma{=}2$, $\alpha{=}0.25$
down-weights the overwhelming easy-negative majority, focusing gradient
updates on hard boundary cases and rare positives.

\paragraph{Sampling}
\texttt{WeightedRandomSampler} with neg:pos ratio of 50:1 ensures
each mini-batch contains approximately 2\% positive samples, compared
to the natural 0.098\%. This is less aggressive than full balancing
(which would oversample positives $\sim$1000$\times$) and reduces
overfitting to the minority class.

\paragraph{Optimiser}
AdamW~\cite{loshchilov2019adamw} with differential learning rates:
the pretrained backbone uses $\text{lr}_\text{backbone} = 5 \times 10^{-5}$
(slow fine-tuning to preserve learned representations), while the
randomly initialised classification head uses
$\text{lr}_\text{head} = 5 \times 10^{-4}$ (10$\times$ faster to
converge quickly). Weight decay is $10^{-4}$.

\paragraph{Learning rate schedule}
OneCycleLR~\cite{smith2019super} with cosine annealing ramps the
learning rate from $\text{lr}/25$ to the peak, then decays to
$\text{lr}/10^4$. This warm-up phase is critical for large pretrained
models to avoid destructive early gradient steps.

\paragraph{Data augmentation}
Training augmentations: horizontal flip, vertical flip, $90^\circ$
rotation, transpose, colour jitter (brightness/contrast $\pm 20\%$,
saturation/hue $\pm 10\%$), Gaussian blur ($\sigma \in [0.1, 2.0]$),
and coarse dropout (max 8 holes, $8 \times 8$ pixels).
All augmentations respect the D4 dihedral symmetry group of lesion
images (no preferred orientation).

\paragraph{GPU strategy}
Smaller models (EfficientNetV2-S, EVA02-Small, 20--22M params)
train on NVIDIA T4 (16~GB), while larger models
(ConvNeXtV2-B, SwinV2-B, 87--88M params) require L40S (48~GB).
Gradient accumulation ($4\times$) is used to simulate larger effective
batch sizes when GPU memory is constrained.

\subsection{Test-Time Augmentation (TTA)}

At inference, each image is transformed by all 8 elements of the
D4 dihedral group (4 rotations $\times$ 2 reflections).
Predictions are averaged in sigmoid (probability) space:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{p}_{\text{TTA}} = \frac{1}{8}\sum_{g \in D_4} \sigma(f_\theta(g \cdot x))
  \label{eq:tta}
\end{equation}
This exploits the fact that lesion orientation is arbitrary in
dermoscopy, and averaging reduces prediction variance without
requiring retraining.

Fig.~\ref{fig:augmentation} shows the 8 D4 transforms applied to a
real lesion image from the training set.

\section{Phase~3: Stacking Meta-Learner}
\label{sec:phase3}

Phase~3 combines predictions from Phase~1 (tabular) and Phase~2
(four image models) through a meta-learner trained on OOF predictions.

\subsection{Feature Construction}

Each model's OOF predictions are rank-transformed to percentile
scores in $[0, 1]$:
\begin{equation}
  r_m(i) = \frac{\text{rank}(\hat{p}_m(i))}{N}
  \label{eq:rank_transform}
\end{equation}
This produces five features:
\texttt{rank\_tabular}, \texttt{rank\_effnet}, \texttt{rank\_eva02},
\texttt{rank\_convnext}, \texttt{rank\_swin}.
Rank transformation is essential because the five base models produce
predictions on different scales (GBDT rank-percentiles, sigmoid
probabilities), and raw values would give undue weight to whichever
scale has larger magnitude.

\subsection{Stacking Methods}

We evaluate four meta-learner approaches with increasing model
complexity:

\paragraph{(1) Rank ensemble (equal weights)}
Simple average of all five rank features, reusing the
\texttt{RankEnsemble} from Phase~1. Zero overfitting risk since
no parameters are learned.

\paragraph{(2) Rank ensemble (pAUC-weighted)}
Weighted average where each model's weight equals its individual
pAUC score, giving more influence to stronger base models.

\paragraph{(3) Logistic regression}
\texttt{LogisticRegression(class\_weight=balanced, C=1.0, solver=lbfgs)}
from scikit-learn, trained per CV fold on rank features.
The linear decision boundary and L2 regularisation provide an
appropriate inductive bias for only 5 features and 393 positives ---
complex nonlinear models cannot be reliably estimated with this
positive count.

\paragraph{(4) Conservative LightGBM}
\texttt{n\_estimators=500}, \texttt{lr=0.01}, \texttt{num\_leaves=7},
\texttt{max\_depth=3}, \texttt{min\_child\_samples=50},
$L_1{=}L_2{=}1.0$, \texttt{scale\_pos\_weight=100}, 50-round early
stopping, averaged over 3 seeds. Despite heavy regularisation, the
GBDT overfits --- confirming that with only 5 features and $\sim$393
positive samples, tree-based nonlinearity is counterproductive.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Why Tabular Outperforms Image Individually}

Phase~1 tabular models (pAUC = 0.1653--0.1672) outperform all four
image models individually (pAUC = 0.1399--0.1549).
This is expected for two reasons:
(1)~the \texttt{tbp\_lv\_*} metadata columns are \emph{quantitative
measurements} of the ABCDE criteria, while image models must learn
to extract these features implicitly from pixel data;
(2)~the ugly duckling z-scores provide patient-relative context that
a single-image model cannot access.
However, image models capture visual patterns (subtle texture, vascular
structures, irregular pigment networks) not represented in the
metadata, as evidenced by the +5.6\% pAUC gain from stacking.

\subsection{Architecture Diversity and Stacking Gains}

The stacking improvement (0.1653 $\to$ 0.1745) confirms that
tabular and image models make complementary errors.
Within image models, the four architectures also contribute differently:
SwinV2-B's shifted-window attention captures multi-scale spatial
relationships, while ConvNeXtV2-B's strong local priors excel at
texture features.
Even the weaker models (EfficientNetV2-S, EVA02-Small) contribute
to the stacking gain because they make \emph{different} errors on
different subsets of samples.

\subsection{Why LogReg Beats LightGBM as Meta-Learner}

With only 393 positive samples and 5 input features, the optimal
meta-learner is the simplest one that can capture the signal.
Logistic regression's linear decision boundary has
$\mathcal{O}(d)$ parameters (5 weights + bias = 6 parameters),
while LightGBM with \texttt{num\_leaves=7} has
$\mathcal{O}(7 \times \text{depth})$ effective parameters per tree
$\times$ 500 trees.
The gap between \texttt{lgbm\_stacker} (0.1645) and
\texttt{logreg\_stacker} (0.1745) is a clear case of the
bias-variance trade-off: the GBDT's additional capacity
\emph{hurts} because there is insufficient positive data to
estimate nonlinear interactions reliably.

\subsection{LightGBM vs.\ XGBoost Imbalance Semantics}

The 0.0486 pAUC gap between LightGBM (0.1217) and XGBoost (0.1703)
in early experiments reflects a fundamental API difference.
LightGBM's \texttt{min\_child\_samples} imposes a count floor that
ignores the boosted weight of positive samples.
Under \texttt{scale\_pos\_weight=100}, a single positive sample
contributes $h \approx 100 \times p(1-p) \approx 25$ hessian weight
(at $p \approx 0.5$), satisfying XGBoost's \texttt{min\_child\_weight=10}
immediately.

\subsection{Fold~3 Difficulty}

Fold~3 is consistently the hardest fold across all models in both
Phase~1 and Phase~2 (Tables~\ref{tab:fold_lgbm}--\ref{tab:phase2_folds}).
Since folds are grouped by patient, this suggests that the patients
in fold~3 have lesions that are inherently harder to classify ---
possibly due to atypical presentations or lower-quality imaging.
This consistent pattern across architecturally diverse models
rules out model-specific bias as the cause.

\subsection{Limitations and Future Work}

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep, leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Hyperparameter search}: GBDT and image model
        hyperparameters were set manually; Bayesian optimisation
        (Optuna) could yield +0.01--0.02 pAUC.
  \item \textbf{Higher resolution}: Image models used 224--336px;
        higher resolution (384--512px) may capture fine-grained
        dermoscopic structures.
  \item \textbf{Advanced augmentation}: MixUp~\cite{zhang2018mixup}
        or CutMix could improve image model generalisation.
  \item \textbf{Patient-level ugly duckling on predictions}:
        Computing rank deviation of each lesion's image prediction
        from the patient mean could provide stacking features that
        capture the ugly duckling signal at the prediction level.
  \item \textbf{Neural tabular models}: TabNet or FT-Transformer
        could complement GBDTs in the tabular phase.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}

We developed a three-phase pipeline for skin lesion classification
on the ISIC~2024 SLICE-3D dataset, progressing from tabular features
(pAUC = 0.1672) through image deep learning (pAUC = 0.1549 best
individual) to a stacking meta-learner achieving
\textbf{pAUC = 0.1745}.

Key findings:
(1)~Tabular metadata with ABCDE feature engineering and ugly duckling
z-scores outperforms image models individually, because the
\texttt{tbp\_lv\_*} columns \emph{are} quantitative ABCDE measurements.
(2)~Image models capture complementary visual signals: stacking
improves pAUC by 5.6\% over the best individual model.
(3)~Architecture diversity (CNN, ViT, ConvNet, hybrid) matters more
than single-model optimisation for stacking ensembles.
(4)~With only 393 positives, logistic regression outperforms LightGBM
as a meta-learner --- a textbook bias-variance trade-off outcome.
(5)~LightGBM's \texttt{min\_child\_samples} is semantically
incompatible with \texttt{scale\_pos\_weight} for imbalanced data.
(6)~Identical CV splits across all phases enable leak-free OOF
alignment, which is the foundation that makes multi-phase stacking
possible.
